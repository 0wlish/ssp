Invasive Plant Management

Invasive plants damage local ecosystems by crowding out native plants, which distrupts food chains. At Habitat, I helped keep these species at bay through weeding. However, non-native does not equal invasive! There were plenty of non-native plants that we did not pull because they weren't harming the ecosystem. Here is a collection of a few plants, native and non-native, that I encountered on this internship.

Yellow Archangel

Introduced in the US, but we didn't manage it at Habitat. In the mint family.

Multiflora Rose

Introduced and invasive in the US. Bane of my existance. A key characteristic of this species is thorns that point downwards. You can imagine that it makes pulling this thing out that much more difficult.

Canada Mayflower

Native to the US. Looks similar to Lily of the Valley, but isn't poisonous.

Hellebore

A genus of spring ephemerals, some native and some non-native. Spring ephemerals are plants that emerge before trees leaf out to take advantage of the increased sunlight in the understory.

Siberian Squill

Another spring ephemeral, non-native but not invasive. One of the first plants to emerge after winter, it is known for its hardy nature.

Lungwort

Introduced but non-invasive in the US. Pretty flowers with spotted leaves.

Eastern Skunk Cabbage

Native to the US, grows in wetlands. Named for the pungent smell that the leaves release when bruised.

Bloodroot

Native to the US. Named for the deep red color of its flesh. Unlike most flowering plants, this plant is pollenated by ants.

Yellow Trout Lily

Anther native spring ephemeral! During the first four to seven years of their life, these plants do not flower and only have one leaf.

Garlic Mustard

Invasive in the US. This plant has few natural predators in North America and is toxic to a few butterfly species. It also inhibits the growth of mycorrhizal fungi. According to Wikipedia, it's also edible. I spent a lot of time pulling this plant up!

Poison Ivy

Native to the US. A climbing vine with leaves in distinctive groups of three. The leaves, stem, and roots of this plant is coated with urushiol, which causes a rash upon contact with skin. However, the goats think it is delicious. Since this plant is native, Habitat doesn't control it unless it is directly on a trail.

Jewelweed

Native to the US, grows in swampy areas. Supposedly counteracts the effects of poison ivy.

Lily of the Valley

Introduced invasive ornamental. Covers large areas of shaded ground. Very pretty, very toxic. Mildly toxic to the goats, but that doesn't stop them from snacking on it.

Lesser Celandine

Another introduced invasive ornamental. This plant sprouts in early spring then uses stored energy to spread quickly and outcompete other plants. It forms large carpets of foliage that look very nice but are aggravatingly difficult to remove. I spent a lot of time pulling out mats of this stuff.

Virginia Creeper

A climbing vine native to the US. This poison ivy lookalike can be distinguished from its toxic neighbor by its groupings of five leaves, instead of three.

Jack-in-the-pulpit

A flower in the arum family. Native to the US.

Other fun things

Though I was mostly managing plants, I kept an eye out for other cool organisms. Here are a few of my favorites.

Wild Turkey

There was a turkey hen that was nesting behind one of our sheds. We counted her eggs when she was off the nest, and there were at least 21.

Common Greenshield Lichen

A very common but very pretty lichen.

Smokey-eyed Boulder Lichen

So named for the pale appearance of its ascomata, which are the lichen's reproductive structures.

Cedar-apple Rust

A plant pathogen fungus that produces large orange galls on cedar trees.

Chlorociboria sp.

A fungus that grows on wood and produces a chemical that stains it blue-green. This is either Chlorociboria aeruginosa or Chlorociboria aeruginascens, it's impossible to tell without a microscope.

White-tailed Deer

I saw these deer chilling just by the path. I must have been twenty feet from them and they were unbothered.

Winter Firefly

These guys were everywhere! They are diurnal, and they do not produce light (despite their name).

Twice-stabbed Lady Beetle

A native ladybug species. The ladybugs you may be familar with were introduced from Asia to control aphid populations. This species eats scale insects.
