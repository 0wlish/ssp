Impact

To fulfill my wellness requirment, I took IMPACT, a self-defense course. The class met twice a week, one class for learning physical skills and one class for discussing the week's reading.

Eeach week we read a chapter from Sexual Citizens, a book summarizing a study done on sexual assaults on college campuses.

Final Paper

Describing IMPACT as a self-defense class doesn't quite tell the whole story. Going into the class, I expected it to focus a lot on technique and drills. Those topics are covered, but what I didn't expect was the opt-in nature of the scenarios, the focus on verbal deescalation, and the emphasis on breathing and knowing how to calm yourself down. To be clear, all these things made it a better class. I went into this class expecting a run-of-the-mill martial arts class, and what I got was an instruction in how to be confident and comfortable in the space you occupy.

A typical class starts with the instructor telling the students what skill they're going to learn and a situation where you might use that skill. The instructor demos the skill with one of the student instructors, who is wearing a large padded suit and a helmet. (This suit was one of the funniest parts of the whole classâ€”imagine football gear with two inches of foam covering every surface. Our instructor demonstrated how she could kick one of the student instructors directly in the groin, and he wouldn't feel a thing.) Then the students slowly learn the steps to the move. The first skill we learned was the eye strike/knee to the groin combo. We learned how to position our hand for the eyestrike, then the form for the knee to the groin, then we put it all together slowly. After that, we did drills with the student instructors where we practiced using the skill on them. Then we moved into scenarios, where the instructor gives us a situation where we would have to use a self-defense skill. Each student would act out the skill with a student instructor while the instructor coached them. This sounded really frightening to me at first, but our instructor explained to us that that's the goal. When you put yourself in an adrenalizing situation, you learn how to properly manage that rush of fear. Nobody was forced to do a scenario, anyone who felt uncomfortable could sit it out. The classes usually ended with breathing exercises and a talk circle. Depending on how much time we had, we learned between one to two skills each class.

The eyestrike and knee to the groin were the two fundamental skills that we built on through different scenarios. We learned how to defend yourself when grabbed from behind (twist, slap the groin, elbow to the face, quarter turn and knee to the groin), how to twist your wrist out of someone's hand (twist your wrist towards the opening and step away), and how to defend yourself if someone grabs you while you're sleeping (wait for an opening, eyestrike, then scootch-kicks on the ground). Most importantly, we also learned verbal skills to deescalate a situation. A creepy guy in a train station is not necessarily a physical threat to you, and if you assume that he is a physical threat before he attacks you, then you've just started an unnecessary fight and put yourself in a murky legal situation. Some of the scenarios we practiced included this added uncertainty. We had to assess the situation and determine if we needed to escalate to a physical altercation. Again, this wasn't karate, we weren't fighting for the fun of it, the goal was alway to avoid a fight. I think these verbal skills were the most important thing I learned in this class.

What I will remember most about this class is the camaraderie that emerges between you and your classmates. We clapped when a classmate finished a drill, and we cheered when she landed a solid hit on the student instructor. I started this class as good friends with some of my classmates and just acquaintances with others, but by the end I felt closer to all of them.

To any junior taking this class, I'd say do it. I took this class because I am an anxious person, and I wanted to feel more confident by knowing I had the skills to defend myself. In this class, I learned not just how to defend myself, but also how to stand up for myself. It may sound stupid, after all standing up for yourself is such a basic skill, but it's something I've never had to do before. Knowing that I had the ability to get loud, to refuse something I didn't want, that was the most helpful thing I learned.
