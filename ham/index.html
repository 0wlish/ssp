Ham radio

For my new experience, I decided to get my ham radio license. Ham radio is a hobby that dates back to the early 1900s, when radio was first invented. Before the Federal Communications Commission (FCC) was established, there was no regulations on radio and the airwaves were filled with hobbyists experimenting with the new technology. When the FCC created the radio band use plan, they allocated space for amateur operators. Today, anyone can take an exam to become licensed to transmit on the ham radio bands. I have wanted to get my license for a while, and I thought that SSP would be the perfect time to do it. I studied for my test for the first part of SSP, and after I got my license, I began constructing a Yagi antenna.

Homemade antenna

After getting my license, I got a Baofeng handheld radio. This radio was better than most handhelds, but it still wasn't good. In order to improve the signals I was receiving and transmitting, I decided to build a directional antenna.

The thing about antennas is that anything can be one if you try hard enough. Well, maybe not anything, but close enough. All I had to do to make a working antenna was feed a coaxial cable to a length of conducting metal then point it in the sky. However, to make a working antenna for a specific frequency, your antenna needs to be a certain length, so it can resonate at that frequency. Things start to get complicated here.

Building the antenna

To start off, I cut three pieces of zinc-plated steel threaded rods and one piece of pine wood. The wood is going to function as the boom (where the elements will be mounted), the two shorter rods will be the driven element, and the longer rod will be the reflector.

Here's the final assembly. After I had the antenna built, I had to find a way to attach the coax. I chopped the end off of a coax cable I bought on amazon, the soldered ring terminals to to the other end. I put the whole thing together to test it.

Finally, I was ready to test it using an antenna analyzer. It's important to have a low standing wave ratio (SWR) in any antenna. SWR measures how much of your signal is being reflected back at your transmitter. Lower is better, 1.2 to 2 is ideal. With some help from the Boston Amateur Radio Club, I tested it with their antenna analyzer...and found that it had an SWR of 10. Something clearly wasn't working! We tested to make sure the cable was working, then I redid the ring terminals and we shortened the elements of the antenna. After a little trial-and-error, I had a SWR of 2. Not bad!
